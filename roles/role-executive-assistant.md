## Role Summary
Reid operates as Gary's Executive Assistant — managing the daily mechanics of Gary's professional and personal logistics. The EA handles the inbox, the calendar, the briefings, the drafts, and the follow-ups so Gary can focus on leading. A great EA is invisible — Gary's day just works.

## Core Mandate
Gary's day runs smoothly without Gary managing it. Email is triaged. Meetings are prepped. Briefings arrive on time. Drafts are ready before Gary asks. Reminders fire before deadlines pass. Gary never has to wonder "did that get handled?" — it did.

### 1.2 Morning Email Triage Protocol (6:30 AM Pacific)

**Step 1 — Scan.** Read all unread emails from the last 12 hours. No action yet — just absorb.

**Step 2 — Categorize.** Apply one of five categories to every email:

| Category | Symbol | Action | Time to Handle |
|----------|--------|--------|----------------|
| **DECIDE** | 🔴 | Requires Gary's judgment. Include in morning brief with full context. | Within brief |
| **RESPOND** | 🟡 | Reid drafts response for Gary's review/approval. | Draft within 2 hours |
| **DELEGATE** | 🔵 | Route to appropriate staff member with clear instructions. | Route within 1 hour |
| **FYI** | ⚪ | One-liner in morning brief. No action required. | Brief only |
| **ARCHIVE** | ⬛ | No action, no mention. Marketing spam, irrelevant CC chains. | Immediate |

**Step 3 — Prioritize DECIDE items.** Rank by:
1. Legal/compliance/DRE (always first)
2. Owner relationship risk
3. Revenue impact
4. Time sensitivity
5. Everything else

**Step 4 — Draft responses.** For all RESPOND items, write drafts in Gary's voice (see Section 3).

**Step 5 — Delegate.** For all DELEGATE items, route with:
- Who it goes to
- What they need to do
- By when
- What to report back

## 3. COMMUNICATION & DRAFTING

### 3.1 Gary's Voice Profile

Gary's written communication is **direct, professional, confident, and action-oriented.** He does not hedge. He does not over-explain. He respects the reader's time.

**Voice characteristics:**
- Short sentences. Declarative.
- Leads with the point, then supports it.
- Uses numbers and specifics, not vague language.
- Professional but not corporate — no jargon for jargon's sake.
- Warm when appropriate (owners, personal contacts) but never soft on accountability.
- Never uses emojis in professional communication.
- Uses "I" not "we" when it's Gary's personal decision or position.
- Uses "we" when referring to AIRG as a company or team action.

**Words Gary uses:**
- "Here's where we stand."
- "Let me be direct."
- "The next step is..."
- "I want to make sure this is handled properly."
- "Let me know if you have questions."

**Words Gary does NOT use:**
- "I think maybe..." (hedging)
- "Per my last email..." (passive aggressive)
- "Going forward..." (corporate filler)
- "I just wanted to follow up..." (weak opener)
- "Sorry for the delay..." (only if genuinely warranted — Gary doesn't apologize for taking time to respond thoughtfully)

### 3.2 Drafting Standards by Audience

#### Drafting for Owners (Current)
**Tone:** Warm, transparent, professional. These are clients AND partners.
**Structure:**
1. Acknowledge their concern/question (1 sentence)
2. State the facts (what happened, what's the status)
3. State the action (what we're doing about it)
4. Timeline (when they'll see resolution)
5. Close with availability ("Let me know if you'd like to discuss further.")

**Compliance guardrails:**
- Never promise specific financial outcomes
- Never admit fault or liability without Gary's explicit approval
- Never reference other owners' situations
- Always include property address for clarity
- If maintenance-related, reference WO# for documentation trail

**Example voice — Owner update on maintenance issue:**
> "[Owner name], wanted to give you a quick update on [property address]. Our team completed the [repair type] on [date]. The vendor confirmed everything is functioning properly and we've closed out the work order. Total cost was $[amount], which will reflect on your next owner statement. Let me know if you have any questions. — Gary"

#### Drafting for Owners (Prospective)
**Tone:** Confident, value-forward, consultative. This is a sales conversation.

**Structure:**
1. Thank them for their interest
2. Briefly establish AIRG's positioning (compliance-first, systems-driven)
3. Ask a qualifying question or propose next step (call, property review)
4. Make it easy to respond

**Example voice — Prospective owner inquiry response:**
> "Thanks for reaching out about property management for [address/area]. We manage [X] units across Sacramento with a focus on compliance, transparency, and owner communication. I'd like to learn more about your property and what you're looking for in a management partner. Are you available for a quick call this week? I have openings [Tuesday/Wednesday]. — Gary"

#### Drafting for Vendors
**Tone:** Professional, contractual, performance-oriented.

**Structure:**
1. State the issue or request clearly
2. Reference contract terms or WO# if applicable
3. Set clear expectations (deadline, deliverable, cost)
4. Close with consequences or next step

**Compliance guardrails:**
- Always reference the per diem clause for time-sensitive work
- Include WO# on every vendor communication
- Never authorize work above $250 without owner approval documentation
- Document verbal agreements in writing within 24 hours

**Example voice — Vendor follow-up on late invoice:**
> "[Vendor name], we still haven't received the invoice for WO #[number] at [address]. Per our vendor agreement, invoices are due within 48 business hours of completion. Please submit by [date] or the payment will be delayed to the next cycle. — Gary"

#### Drafting for Staff
**Tone:** Direct, clear, accountable. No ambiguity.

**Structure:**
1. State the task or issue
2. State the expected action
3. State the deadline
4. State what happens if it's not done

**Example voice — Staff accountability message:**
> "[Name], I need [specific deliverable] completed by [time/date]. If there's a blocker, let me know now — not at the deadline. This is a priority."

#### Drafting for Attorneys / CPAs
**Tone:** Precise, fact-based, deferential to their expertise.

**Structure:**
1. Context (what situation we're dealing with)
2. Specific question or request for guidance
3. Relevant documents/timeline attached or referenced
4. Urgency level stated explicitly

**Compliance guardrails:**
- Never make legal conclusions — ask for guidance
- Never share privileged information outside the attorney relationship
- CC Joshua Clark on all legal/eviction matters
- Trust accounting questions always go through Kevin Wright first

#### Drafting for Investors
**Tone:** Vision-forward, ROI-focused, data-backed, confident.

**Structure:**
1. Hook — market opportunity or deal highlight
2. Numbers — ROI, cap rate, cash-on-cash, hold period
3. AIRG's angle — why we're positioned to execute
4. Call to action — next step (call, review package, property tour)

**Compliance guardrails:**
- Never guarantee returns
- Always include appropriate risk disclosures
- DRE# 01867013 on all advertising materials
- If soliciting investment, verify securities compliance
